GLM-5.2 vs Claude Opus 4.8 vs GPT-5.5 for Enterprise Coding

The benchmark tables circulating since GLM-5.2's launch mix numbers that were never measured the same way. Here is what the scores can and cannot tell you — and how the three models actually differ for an engineering organisation.

← All posts

Since Z.ai released GLM-5.2 on 13 June, every comparison article has led with the same table: 62.1 on SWE-bench Pro against GPT-5.5's 58.6, and 74.4 percent on FrontierSWE against 75.1 for Claude Opus 4.8 and 72.6 for GPT-5.5. The numbers are real. The problem is that they were not all produced the same way, and the difference matters more than the decimal points.

The short answer: Claude Opus 4.8 remains the quality ceiling for complex coding work. GLM-5.2 gets within a point of it on FrontierSWE at roughly a sixth of the price, and is the first open-weight model to beat a US frontier flagship on agent-style software engineering benchmarks. GPT-5.5 trails both on these specific tests but remains the most widely deployed of the three. Whether any of that translates to your team depends on questions the benchmarks do not answer.

This piece covers the capability comparison. The governance decision — API versus self-hosted weights versus abstaining — is the subject of our decision guide to open-weight frontier models, and the UK compliance mechanics are in our data-residency brief.

Read the Benchmark Numbers Sceptically

Most published SWE-bench Pro scores are vendor self-reported figures produced on tuned agent scaffolds. Scale AI's standardised leaderboard, which runs every model on identical scaffolding, tells a humbler story: the aggregated tracking of SWE-bench Pro results notes that while the vendor aggregate credits Opus 4.8 with 69.2 percent, Scale's best standardised Claude run scored 51.9 percent on the public set. A score 10 to 30 points above the standardised leaderboard is a scaffold number, not a model number.

GLM-5.2's own 62.1 carries a further caveat: it was measured by third parties, because Z.ai published no SWE-bench figure at launch. So the honest reading of the table is narrower than the headlines: GLM-5.2 demonstrably beats GPT-5.5 on like-for-like runs, and sits within a point of Opus 4.8 on FrontierSWE, where the scores come from the same analysis. The 69.2-versus-62.1 comparison between Opus and GLM crosses harnesses and should not be quoted as a gap of seven points.

For an enterprise evaluation, the practical rule: trust orderings within a single standardised run, treat cross-source comparisons as directional, and pilot on your own repositories before believing any of it.

What Each Model Offers an Engineering Organisation

GLM-5.2: frontier capability at commodity price

The economics are the headline: $1.40 per million input tokens and $4.40 output, with cached input at $0.26, against $5/$25 for Opus 4.8 (llm-stats comparison). For token-heavy agentic workloads — long refactors, whole-repository analysis using the 1M-token context — the gap compounds daily.

The under-reported fact is how little switching costs. Z.ai exposes an Anthropic-compatible API endpoint for coding tools, so GLM-5.2 drops into Claude Code, Cline, and Cursor by changing a base URL and key — in Claude Code the 1M-context variant is glm-5.2[1m] (setup guide). Teams that have invested in agentic tooling around Claude, as many covered in our coding tool comparison have, can trial GLM-5.2 inside the same workflow in an afternoon. The same compatibility cuts the other way: adopting GLM-5.2 creates no tooling lock-in.

The weaknesses are the mirror of the price. There is no vendor with a UK legal presence standing behind the output, the benchmark record is thinner and partly third-party measured, and the data-residency analysis has to be done before any pilot touches real code. Early adopter reports also note migration friction — model-id conventions differ by tool, and routing through intermediary providers has produced tool-call errors in Cursor.

Claude Opus 4.8: the quality ceiling, priced accordingly

Opus 4.8 leads FrontierSWE at 75.1 percent and is the model the others are measured against — the strongest choice where correctness on complex, multi-file work dominates cost. The ecosystem argument compounds the model argument: Claude Code remains the most invested-in agentic coding tool, with a 46 percent "most loved" rating in The Pragmatic Engineer's survey of 906 developers, and the co-design advantage of tool and model coming from the same lab shows up in community reports on output quality.

The cost is real: at $5/$25 per million tokens, an Opus-heavy team pays for that margin continuously, whether or not a given task needed it. The economical pattern we see is a split estate — Opus for planning, architecture, and the hardest debugging; a cheaper model for volume work. Until June, the cheaper tier meant Haiku- or Sonnet-class models. GLM-5.2's arrival within a point of Opus changes what "cheaper tier" can mean.

GPT-5.5: behind on these benchmarks, ahead on distribution

GPT-5.5 trails on both coding benchmarks — 58.6 on SWE-bench Pro and 72.6 percent on FrontierSWE against GLM-5.2's 62.1 and 74.4. But coding benchmarks are not the whole of engineering work: as we covered in our analysis of GPT-5.5's autonomous task completion, its strength is sustained multi-step task execution, and it remains the default model in more enterprise tooling than either rival. For organisations standardised on OpenAI through Microsoft's ecosystem, the switching cost runs the other way: the question is not whether GPT-5.5 tops a leaderboard but whether the gap justifies leaving an integrated estate.

Cost per Token Is Not Cost per Task

The sixfold price gap is a per-token figure, and engineering organisations do not buy tokens — they buy completed work. If a cheaper model needs two attempts where a stronger one needs one, the gap halves before you account for the engineer's time reviewing the failed attempt. On FrontierSWE the quality difference between GLM-5.2 and Opus 4.8 is 0.7 points; on your codebase it may be larger or smaller, and no public retry-adjusted cost data yet exists for GLM-5.2. That is measurable in a two-week pilot, and worth measuring: at these list prices, even a modest real-world quality discount leaves GLM-5.2 far cheaper per completed task, while a large one erases the saving entirely.

Caching shifts the same calculation. Agentic coding re-reads the same files continuously, and GLM-5.2's $0.26 cached-input rate — like the caching economics we noted for Claude Code in our tool comparison — means real workloads run materially below list price on both sides of the comparison.

Which Model for Which Team

  • Complex work where correctness dominates cost. Opus 4.8, and it is not close once ecosystem depth is counted. The premium is the point.
  • High-volume agentic workloads with cost pressure. Pilot GLM-5.2 through the Anthropic-compatible endpoint inside your existing tooling — after the governance analysis in our decision guide, not before.
  • Microsoft-standardised estates. GPT-5.5's benchmark deficit is unlikely to justify the integration cost of leaving; revisit when your contract does.
  • Whole-repository analysis. GLM-5.2 and Opus 4.8 both offer a 1M-token window; GPT-5.5 runs at roughly 400K. GLM-5.2 is the one priced for filling that window routinely — a full-context call at $25 per million output tokens is an occasional tool, not a workflow.

The durable pattern is not a winner but a portfolio: a frontier US model where accountability and peak quality matter, and an open-weight model where volume, context length, or data control matter. GLM-5.2's contribution is making the second slot frontier-grade.
